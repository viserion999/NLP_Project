\begin{abstract}
Creative language generation has gained increasing attention in natural language 
processing, particularly for tasks such as poetry and song lyric generation. 
However, most existing systems focus either on structural constraints such as 
rhyme and meter or on semantic control mechanisms such as sentiment, topic, 
or style. The integration of multimodal emotional signals with rhythm-aware 
lyric generation remains relatively underexplored.

In this work, we propose \textbf{MERGE} (Multimodal Emotion-Driven Rhythmic 
Lyric Generation Engine), a framework for generating rhythm-aware song lyrics 
conditioned on emotional states inferred from facial expressions. The system 
first detects the user’s emotional state using a facial emotion recognition 
model and then conditions GPT-2 on the predicted 
emotion to guide lyric generation. To maintain lyrical structure, we employ 
a rhythm-aware reranking strategy that selects candidate lines based on 
syllable counts and rhyme patterns.

We further introduce \textbf{PHREM}, a composite evaluation metric designed 
for lyric generation that measures rhythmic consistency, emotional alignment, 
lexical diversity, and semantic coherence. MERGE provides a framework for 
exploring emotion-conditioned lyric generation while preserving structural 
properties of song lyrics.\end{abstract}




\section{Introduction}

The motivation of this project is to develop an emotion-aware system 
capable of generating creative musical content based on a user's 
emotional state inferred from sensory inputs. Direct music synthesis 
conditioned on physiological or multimodal sensor data poses significant 
challenges due to limited dataset availability, the high dimensionality 
of audio generation, and the complex alignment between emotional intent 
and musical structure \cite{cao2022multi}. To preserve the objective of 
emotion-driven creative generation while remaining within the methodological 
scope of natural language processing (NLP), this work reformulates the 
problem as emotion-conditioned lyric generation.

Facial emotion recognition provides a practical and widely studied 
modality for sensing emotional states \cite{goodfellow2013challenges, li2017reliable}.
 At the same time, song lyrics serve as a primary textual medium through which 
 emotional expression is conveyed in music. These properties make lyrics a 
 suitable domain for studying emotion-controlled and structurally constrained 
 language generation. Prior research in NLP has explored emotion-conditioned 
 text generation as well as structural constraints such as rhyme schemes and 
 syllable patterns in poetry and lyric generation. However, these directions 
 have largely been investigated independently.

This work leverages large-scale lyric corpora 
obtained from the Genius lyric dataset \cite{lim2021expertise}, which 
provides substantially larger collections of song lyrics. Since explicit 
emotion annotations are not available for this dataset, we construct 
emotion labels using a scalable annotation pipeline that aggregates 
predictions from a GoEmotions-based classifier and maps fine-grained 
emotional categories into the six facial emotion recognition (FER) classes 
through normalization and weighted line-level aggregation. This procedure 
enables the creation of large-scale emotion-labeled lyric datasets suitable 
for training emotion-conditioned language models.

While prior work has examined controlled text generation and rhythm-aware 
lyric generation separately, the integration of facial emotion signals as 
control inputs for rhythm-aware lyric generation remains relatively 
underexplored, particularly in English-language songwriting. Bridging 
this gap requires a framework that can incorporate visual emotional 
cues into the generation process while maintaining the structural 
characteristics that define song lyrics.

In this work, we propose 
\textbf{MERGE (Multimodal Emotion-Driven Rhythmic Lyric Generation Engine)}, 
a multimodal framework that connects facial emotion recognition with 
rhythm-aware lyric generation. MERGE uses facial emotion recognition 
models to infer emotional states from visual inputs and employs these 
signals to condition a transformer-based language model trained on 
large-scale lyric corpora. To ensure structural coherence, 
the generation process incorporates rhythm-aware decoding constraints
 based on syllable counts and rhyme schemes.

This work presents MERGE, a multimodal framework for emotion-conditioned 
lyric generation that integrates facial emotion recognition with rhythm-aware 
text generation.

The contributions of this work are fourfold: (1) MERGE, a unified framework 
that connects facial emotion recognition with lyric generation; (2) a scalable 
emotion annotation pipeline that maps fine-grained GoEmotions predictions to FER 
categories using normalization and weighted aggregation, enabling large-scale 
labeling of lyric corpora; (3) a rhythm-aware reranking strategy that promotes 
syllabic and rhyming structure during generation; and (4) PHREM, a composite 
evaluation metric for assessing rhythmic consistency, emotional alignment, and overall lyric quality.

The source code and implementation details for this project are available
 at the following link.\footnote{\url{https://github.com/IIITH-2025-27/NLP_Project}}


\section{Related Work}

\textbf{Facial Emotion Recognition.}
Facial emotion recognition has achieved strong performance using deep 
convolutional neural networks, with architectures such as ResNet 
and MobileNet commonly evaluated on benchmark datasets including 
FER-2013 and RAF-DB \cite{goodfellow2013challenges, li2017reliable}. 
The FER-2013 dataset contains over 35,000 grayscale facial images 
annotated with basic emotion categories and remains a widely used 
benchmark due to its scale and accessibility \cite{goodfellow2013challenges}. 
Recent work has explored transformer-based architectures and multimodal 
fusion methods, improving the modeling of contextual emotional signals 
\cite{zhang2023multimodal}. These advances make facial emotion recognition 
a reliable component for downstream generative systems.

\textbf{Controlled Text Generation.}
Controlled text generation aims to steer language models toward desired 
attributes such as sentiment, topic, or style. Early approaches relied 
on conditional fine-tuning and control tokens \cite{radford2019language}, 
while more recent methods explore attribute-guided generation for aligning 
outputs with semantic properties such as emotional tone \cite{hu2023attribute}. 
These approaches demonstrate that external control signals can effectively 
guide generative language models.

\textbf{Lyric and Poetry Generation.}
Prior work on poetry and lyric generation has focused on enforcing structural 
constraints such as rhyme, meter, and syllable patterns. Early approaches 
used rule-based methods and phonetic resources like the CMU Pronouncing 
Dictionary \cite{ghazvininejad2016generating, gonccalo2024automatic}. 
Neural models later incorporated such constraints to produce more coherent 
poetic text. Recent systems further integrate musical or prosodic signals 
to guide generation. For instance, SongRewriter \cite{sun2023songrewriter} 
uses multi-level masking for controllable lyric generation, while 
XAI-Lyricist \cite{liang2024xai} incorporates prosody to improve 
singability and interpretability. However, these approaches primarily 
focus on structural or musical guidance and do not explicitly study 
the interaction between rhythmic constraints and emotion-conditioned 
generation.

\textbf{Collaborative and Multimodal Generation Systems.}
Recent work has explored multi-agent and multimodal approaches for lyric 
generation. Liu et al. \cite{liu2024agent} propose a multi-agent system 
for handling rhyme, syllable control, and alignment tasks, while Ram 
et al. \cite{ram2021say} use multitask learning with T5 to model lyrical 
constraints. More broadly, multimodal generative models such as MusicLM 
\cite{huang2023musiclm} demonstrate the potential of combining semantic 
signals with generative models, although they primarily focus on audio 
generation rather than lyrics.

Despite these advances, existing systems either emphasize structural 
generation or rely on musical signals such as melody and prosody. The 
integration of visual emotion signals such as facial expressions with 
rhythm-aware lyric generation remains underexplored, particularly in 
English-language songwriting. MERGE addresses this gap by providing a framework that connects facial emotion recognition with 
rhythm-aware lyric generation.

\subsection{Dataset Construction and Emotion Mapping}
\label{sec:dataset}

Large-scale lyric datasets typically lack explicit emotion 
annotations, which makes it difficult to train emotion-conditioned 
language models. To address this limitation, we construct an 
emotion-labeled lyric dataset using an automatic annotation pipeline 
as shown in Figure \ref{fig:dataset_pipeline}.




Lyrics are collected from large public lyric corpora, specifically the Genius dataset \cite{lim2021expertise}, which contains lyrics in many languages out of which approx 3.2 million belongs to English language. Emotional signals are inferred using a pretrained emotion classifier based on the RoBERTa architecture\cite{lowe2023roberta}, which is fine-tuned on the GoEmotions dataset \cite{demszky2020goemotions}. The GoEmotions label space contains $28$ categories, consisting of $27$ fine-grained emotion labels and one neutral category. For each input lyric line, the classifier outputs a probability distribution over these $28$ labels, which serves as the basis for constructing the emotion representations used in the subsequent stages of the pipeline.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{diagram2.pdf}
\caption{Pipeline for constructing the emotion-labeled lyric dataset.}
\label{fig:dataset_pipeline}
\end{figure}
\vspace{-0.3cm}

To ensure compatibility between textual emotions and facial emotion recognition outputs, the fine-grained emotion space is mapped into a smaller set of basic emotions, inspired by the circumplex model of affect and theories of basic emotions \cite{russell1980circumplex, ekman1992argument}. The emotion space is reduced to the following set
\[
\mathcal{E} =
\{\text{angry}, \text{fear}, \text{happy}, \text{sad}, \text{surprise}, \text{neutral}\}.
\]

% \vspace{-0.7cm}


The disgust category is excluded for two reasons. First, explicit expressions of disgust rarely occur in song lyrics and are often linguistically expressed as sadness or disappointment. Second, the FER-2013 facial emotion dataset contains very few samples labeled as disgust, resulting in severe class imbalance during model training. To maintain a consistent emotion space across modalities, disgust-related signals are therefore merged into the sadness category.
The created dataset is being hosted on kaggle\cite{author2025}.



\subsection{Lyric Dataset and Emotion Labeling}
Each song is segmented into individual lyric lines. Let a song contain $n$ lyric lines, where the index of a line is denoted by
\vspace{-0.25cm}
\[
i \in \{1,2,\dots,n\}.
\]


For each lyric line $i$, the GoEmotions classifier produces a probability vector over the $28$ emotion labels:
\[
\mathbf{p}_i =
\left[
p_i^{(1)}, p_i^{(2)}, \dots, p_i^{(28)}
\right] \in \mathbb{R}^{28},
\]
where $p_i^{(e)}$ denotes the probability assigned to label $e$ and satisfies
\[
0 \le p_i^{(e)} \le 1.
\]

To obtain emotion representations compatible with facial emotion recognition categories, the fine-grained emotion labels are grouped into six emotion sets:
\[
\begin{aligned}
A &= \{\text{anger}, \text{annoyance}, \text{disapproval}\}, \\
F &= \{\text{fear}, \text{nervousness}\}, \\
H &= \{\text{admiration}, \text{amusement}, \text{approval}, \text{caring}, \\
  &\quad \text{desire}, \text{excitement}, \text{gratitude}, \text{joy}, \text{optimism}, \\
  &\quad \text{love}, \text{pride}, \text{relief}\}, \\
S &= \{\text{sadness}, \text{grief}, \text{disappointment}, \text{remorse}, \\
  &\quad \text{disgust}\}, \\
U &= \{\text{surprise}, \text{realization}\}, \\
N &= \{\text{neutral}, \text{curiosity}, \text{confusion}\}.
\end{aligned}
\]

Using these groups, the emotion score for each category is computed by summing probabilities belonging to the same group. For example, the anger score for line $i$ is defined as
\[
q_i^{\text{angry}} =
\sum_{e \in A} p_i^{(e)}.
\]

Applying the same procedure to all emotion groups produces a six-dimensional emotion vector for lyric line $i$:
\[
\mathbf{q}_i =
\left[
q_i^{\text{angry}},
q_i^{\text{fear}},
q_i^{\text{happy}},
q_i^{\text{sad}},
q_i^{\text{surprise}},
q_i^{\text{neutral}}
\right].
\]

Because multiple probabilities are aggregated, the resulting vector is normalized to obtain a valid probability distribution:
\[
\mathbf{q}_i =
\frac{\mathbf{q}_i}
{\sum_{k=1}^{6} q_i^{(k)}}.
\]

Lyrics often contain narrative lines with little emotional content. To reduce their influence, each line is assigned an emotional weight defined as
\[
w_i = 1 - q_i^{\text{neutral}},
\]
where $w_i$ represents the emotional intensity of lyric line $i$.

The final emotion representation of a song is computed by aggregating the line-level emotion vectors using weighted averaging:
\[
\mathbf{E}_{\text{song}}
=
\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i \mathbf{q}_i}
{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i}.
\]

The dominant emotion of the song is defined as
\[
e^* =
\arg\max_k
\mathbf{E}_{\text{song}}^{(k)},
\]
where $\mathbf{E}_{\text{song}}^{(k)}$ denotes the score of emotion category $k$.

\section{Methodology}
% ============================================================
This work proposes an end-to-end multimodal framework that connects 
facial emotion recognition with emotion-conditioned and 
rhythm-aware lyric generation.

The system

emotional states from facial inputs and then uses these signals to

\vspace{-0.3cm}





\subsection{Facial Emotion Conditioning}

The MERGE framework uses facial expressions as the emotional input signal for lyric generation. Given a facial image, a facial emotion recognition model predicts the emotional state of the user (as shown in Figure \ref{fig:vision_pipeline}), which is then used to condition the lyric generation process.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{diagram3.pdf}
\caption{Pipeline of the facial emotion recognition module.}
\label{fig:vision_pipeline}
\end{figure}

The facial emotion recognition model is trained on the datasets described in Section \ref{facial_expression_datasets}. 


Let $I$ denote the input facial image and let $f_{\theta}$ represent the facial emotion recognition model parameterized by $\theta$. The model produces a vector of logits

\[
\mathbf{z} = f_{\theta}(I)
\]

where $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^{6}$ represents the unnormalized scores corresponding to the six emotion categories.

The probability distribution over emotions is computed using the softmax function

\[
P(e_k \mid I) =
\frac{\exp(z_k)}
{\sum_{j=1}^{6} \exp(z_j)}
\]

where $e_k \in \mathcal{E}$ denotes the $k$-th emotion category and $z_k$ represents the corresponding logit value.

The predicted facial emotion is obtained by selecting the class with the highest probability

\[
e^{*} =
\arg\max_{e_k \in \mathcal{E}}
P(e_k \mid I)
\]

The predicted emotion $e^{*}$ is then used as the conditioning signal for the lyric generation model. During inference, the specified emotion is encoded as a control token (for example \texttt{\textless sad\textgreater}) and prepended to the input sequence of the language model. This allows the model to generate lyrics that are aligned with the emotional state inferred from the user's facial expression.


% \vspace{0.25cm}

\subsection{Emotion-Conditioned Language Model}

Lyric generation is formulated as a controlled text generation task in which emotional context influences the semantic content of generated lyrics. We fine-tune the GPT-2 language model \cite{radford2019language} on the emotion-labeled lyric dataset constructed in the previous subsection.

Emotion conditioning is implemented using explicit control tokens representing the target emotion category. During training, an emotion token (for example \texttt{\textless sad\textgreater}) is prepended to each lyric sequence so that the model can learn associations between emotional states and linguistic patterns.

Let
\[
x = (x_1, x_2, \dots, x_T)
\]
denote a lyric token sequence of length $T$, and let $e$ represent the conditioning emotion label. The model learns the conditional probability
\[
P(x \mid e)
=
\prod_{t=1}^{T}
P(x_t \mid x_{<t}, e),
\]
where $x_t$ denotes the token generated at step $t$ and $x_{<t}$ represents the previously generated tokens.

The model is trained using the standard cross-entropy objective for next-token prediction.

\subsection{Rhythm-Aware Reranking}

To encourage rhythmic and rhyming structure during lyric generation, we employ a rhythm-aware reranking strategy. The CMU Pronouncing Dictionary provides phonetic representations that enable syllable counting and rhyme detection \cite{cmudict}.

Given an input emotion, multiple candidate lyric lines are generated using stochastic sampling. Each candidate is then evaluated using a scoring function that incorporates both syllabic meter and rhyme consistency.

Syllable counts are computed using CMU-based pronunciations when available, with a fallback heuristic for out-of-vocabulary words. A meter score penalizes deviations from a predefined target syllable count. Formally, given a line $L$ and target syllable count $T$, the meter score is defined as:
\[
\text{MeterScore}(L) = - \left| \sum_{w \in L} \text{syllables}(w) - T \right|.
\]

Rhyme consistency is evaluated using phonetic rhyme segments derived from CMU pronunciations. A binary rhyme score is assigned based on whether the final word of a candidate line matches the rhyme pattern of a reference word:
\[
\text{RhymeScore}(L) =
\begin{cases}
1, & \text{if rhyme matches} \\
0, & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\]

The overall score is computed as a weighted combination of meter and rhyme:
\[
\text{Score}(L) = \text{MeterScore}(L) + \lambda \cdot \text{RhymeScore}(L),
\]
where $\lambda$ controls the relative importance of rhyme (set to 5 in our implementation).

Additionally, low-quality candidates are filtered using simple heuristics that remove 
excessively short, repetitive, or filler-dominated lines.

The final output is selected as the highest-scoring candidate, enabling 
the model to produce rhythmically and phonetically structured lyrics without 
modifying the underlying decoding process. This formulation prioritizes 
rhythmic consistency while softly encouraging rhyme structure, allowing 
flexibility when strict constraint satisfaction is not achievable.

\section{Evaluation Metric: PHREM}
% ============================================================

Existing metrics such as BLEU and ROUGE measure lexical overlap but are 
insufficient for evaluating song lyrics, which require assessment of phonetic 
flow, rhythmic consistency, emotional coherence, and semantic structure. We 
introduce \textbf{PHREM} (Phonetic Rhythm Emotion Metric), a composite 
evaluation framework designed for lyric quality assessment in the context of 
emotion-conditioned generation.

PHREM integrates seven complementary components, each targeting a distinct 
dimension of lyric quality. The base score is computed as a weighted sum:

\begin{equation}
\text{PHREM} = \sum_{i=1}^{7} \alpha_i c_i
\end{equation}

where weights are: $\alpha_{\text{ppfs}}=0.12$, $\alpha_{\text{rsfs}}=0.13$, 
$\alpha_{\text{eas}}=0.22$, $\alpha_{\text{hqcs}}=0.13$, 
$\alpha_{\text{rcs}}=0.10$, $\alpha_{\text{mcs}}=0.20$, $\alpha_{\text{dps}}=0.10$. 
All components are normalized to $[0,1]$, ensuring 
$\text{PHREM}_{\text{final}} \in [0,1]$. Component weights were determined 
through human judgment, where annotators assessed the relative contribution 
of each dimension to overall lyric quality.

\paragraph{PPFS: Phonetic Pattern Flow Score.}
PPFS evaluates phoneme-level smoothness by penalizing abrupt vowel-consonant 
transitions and stress mismatches between adjacent phonemes. Phoneme sequences 
are extracted using the CMU Pronouncing Dictionary, with G2P as a fallback for 
out-of-vocabulary words. For adjacent phonemes $p_i$ and $p_{i+1}$:

\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
c(p_i, p_{i+1}) = \; & 0.6 \cdot \mathbf{1}[v(p_i) \neq v(p_{i+1})] \\
                    + \; & 0.4 \cdot |s_i - s_{i+1}|
\end{split}
\end{equation}

where $v(p)$ indicates whether phoneme $p$ is a vowel, $\mathbf{1}[\cdot]$ indicates a vowel-consonant transition, and 
$|s_i - s_{i+1}|$ captures stress mismatch. The final score is 
$\text{PPFS} = \exp(-\bar{c})$, where $\bar{c}$ is the mean transition cost 
across all adjacent phoneme pairs.

\paragraph{RSFS: Rhythmic Structure Flow Score.}
RSFS measures rhythmic consistency across lines by evaluating syllable count 
uniformity and stress pattern regularity. Syllable consistency is measured 
relative to the median line length:

\begin{equation}
R_s = 1 - \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{|s_i - \text{med}(s)|}{\text{med}(s)}
\end{equation}

Stress consistency $R_{\text{stress}}$ is computed by comparing stress 
sequences of consecutive lines over their overlapping syllable positions. 
The final score is $\text{RSFS} = 0.7 R_s + 0.3 R_{\text{stress}}$, 
weighting syllable uniformity more heavily.

\paragraph{EAS: Emotion Arc Score.}
EAS measures emotional consistency across the lyric sequence. For each line 
$i$, a RoBERTa-based GoEmotions classifier produces a probability vector that 
is mapped and normalized to six FER emotion categories, yielding 
$\mathbf{q}_i \in [0,1]^6$. Transitions between consecutive lines are measured 
using Jensen-Shannon divergence:

\begin{equation}
d_i = \frac{1}{2}\left[\text{KL}(\mathbf{q}_i \| \mathbf{m}) + 
\text{KL}(\mathbf{q}_{i+1} \| \mathbf{m})\right]
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{m} = \frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{q}_i + \mathbf{q}_{i+1})$. Each 
transition is weighted by emotional intensity $w_i = 1 - q_i^{\text{neutral}}$ 
to reduce the influence of narratively neutral lines. The final score is 
$\text{EAS} = \exp(-\bar{d}_w)$, where $\bar{d}_w$ is the weighted mean 
divergence. Lower divergence between consecutive lines yields higher scores, 
rewarding smooth emotional progression. EAS receives the highest weight 
($\alpha_{\text{eas}} = 0.22$) as emotional coherence is central to the 
generation objective of MERGE.

\paragraph{HQCS: Hook Quality and Catchiness Score.}
HQCS rewards the presence of repeated $n$-gram patterns that contribute to 
memorability. All 3-grams are extracted and those appearing more than once are 
treated as candidate hooks. The score combines salience (maximum hook frequency 
relative to total $n$-grams), coverage (total hook occurrences relative to 
total $n$-grams), and flow quality (mean of PPFS and RSFS):

\begin{equation}
\text{HQCS} = 0.4 \cdot \text{sal} + 0.3 \cdot \text{cov} + 0.3 \cdot \text{flow}
\end{equation}

A diversity penalty $\exp(-k \cdot (1 - \text{diversity}))$ is incorporated 
to prevent degenerate repetition from being rewarded. When no repeated $n$-grams 
are present, HQCS falls back to the mean of PPFS and RSFS.

\paragraph{RCS: Rhyme Consistency Score.}
RCS evaluates end-rhyme quality by comparing the phonetic endings of 
consecutive line-final words. The rhyme region is defined from the last 
stressed vowel onward, extracted using the CMU Pronouncing Dictionary. 
Similarity between consecutive rhyme sequences is computed by matching 
phonemes backward from the word ending. RCS is the mean similarity across 
all consecutive line pairs. We note that this approach captures direct 
phonetic rhyme but does not model rhyme scheme structure (e.g., AABB, ABAB) 
or slant rhymes.

\paragraph{MCS: Motif Consistency Score.}
MCS measures thematic coherence across lines using sentence embeddings from 
\texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2}. Both local coherence (between adjacent lines) and 
global coherence (across sampled non-adjacent pairs) are considered:

\begin{equation}
\text{MCS} = 0.7 \cdot L + 0.3 \cdot G
\end{equation}

where $L$ is the mean cosine similarity of adjacent line embeddings and $G$ 
is the mean cosine similarity of a random sample of non-adjacent pairs. 
This formulation rewards lyrics that maintain a consistent topic or theme 
throughout, complementing EAS which focuses on emotional rather than 
semantic continuity.

\paragraph{DPS: Degeneracy Penalty Score.}
DPS penalizes excessive lexical repetition, which is a common failure mode 
in autoregressive language model outputs. The penalty is defined as:

\begin{equation}
\text{DPS} = \exp(-3.0 \cdot r)
\end{equation}

where $r = \frac{\text{max word frequency}}{\text{total words}}$. This 
decays sharply when a single word dominates the output, while allowing 
natural repetition in well-formed lyrics.

\paragraph{LPS: Length Penalty Score.}
LPS acts as a multiplicative gate to penalize outputs that are too short to 
constitute meaningful lyrics. Very short outputs may achieve artificially 
high scores on individual components due to limited evidence. The penalty is:

\begin{equation}
\text{LPS} = \begin{cases} 1.0 & \text{if } n \geq 8 \\ 
\left(\dfrac{n}{8}\right)^{2} & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}
\end{equation}

where $n$ is the number of lyric lines. The final PHREM score is obtained by 
multiplying the weighted sum by LPS:

\begin{equation}
\text{PHREM}_{\text{final}} = \text{PHREM} \times \text{LPS}
\end{equation}

This multiplicative formulation ensures that structural deficiencies 
(insufficient length) override component-level scores rather than being 
diluted by averaging. The threshold of $n = 8$ lines and steepness of 2.0 
are chosen as reasonable priors for a minimal song verse; these 
hyperparameters were not empirically tuned. All components operate on 
$[0,1]$ scales, making PHREM directly interpretable as a normalized 
lyric quality score.

\section{Experiments and Training Setup}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Facial Emotion Recognition}

To improve generalization, DenseNet-121 is trained on a unified corpus 
comprising FER-2013 (35{,}887 images), RAF-DB ($\sim$15{,}000 images), 
and AffectNet ($\sim$30{,}000 images) mapped into a six-class label space 
with 80/10/10 split and MTCNN face filtering \cite{zhang2016joint}. 
The training set is expanded using domain-bridging augmentations 
(RandomGrayscale $p=0.3$, GaussianBlur, ColorJitter, RandomAffine, 
RandomPerspective) for improved generalization across datasets. DenseNet-121 
is trained on Kaggle GPU resources with progressive unfreezing over 30 epochs 
(5 warmup, Cosine Annealing scheduler, head first then backbone lr $=10^{-4}$, 
head lr $=10^{-3}$), Focal Loss \cite{lin2017focal} ($\gamma=2$) with label 
smoothing ($\varepsilon=0.1$). Final predictions employ test-time augmentation 
to generate multiple prediction sets, which are then aggregated via averaging 
to improve robustness and generalization across the unified dataset.

\subsection{Language Model Fine-Tuning}

A GPT-2 language model was fine-tuned on an emotion-labeled lyric dataset using a control-token-based conditioning strategy. Each training example was prepended with a special emotion token (e.g., \texttt{<sad>}, \texttt{<happy>}) to enable controllable generation within a single unified model.

The model was trained using the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of $5 \times 10^{-5}$ for 3 epochs. Training was performed with a batch size of 32 and a maximum sequence length of 120 tokens. To ensure balanced learning across emotions, the dataset was sampled to include up to 60,000 examples per emotion class.

During inference, lyric lines are generated using nucleus (top-$p$) sampling with $p=0.9$ and a temperature of 0.85. Additional decoding constraints include a repetition penalty of 1.25 and a no-repeat n-gram size of 3 to reduce redundancy.

To further encourage rhythmic and rhyming structure, a rhythm-aware reranking mechanism is applied over multiple sampled candidates. Specifically, 5 candidate lines are generated per step and scored based on syllabic meter and rhyme consistency. The target meter is set to 8 syllables per line, and rhyme alignment is weighted using a coefficient $\lambda = 5$. The candidate with the highest combined score is selected as the final output.

\section{Analysis}

\textbf{Effectiveness of Emotion Conditioning.}
Emotion conditioning through control tokens produces clearly distinguishable 
lyric outputs across all six emotion categories. Qualitative inspection shows 
consistent thematic variation, with \texttt{<happy>} outputs favoring positive 
and celebratory language, while \texttt{<sad>} outputs reflect themes of loss 
and introspection. This indicates that control-token-based conditioning effectively 
guides semantic generation.

\textbf{Impact of Dataset Quality.}
The baseline comparison using PHREM demonstrates that fine-tuning on the proposed 
dataset significantly improves generation quality, achieving an average score of 
0.55 compared to 0.146 for Spotify-based fine-tuning and 0.03 for the pretrained 
model. This substantial improvement highlights the importance of large-scale, 
emotion-aligned supervision. The proposed annotation pipeline enables stronger 
alignment between textual content and emotional labels, leading to improved 
emotional coherence and structural quality in generated lyrics.

\textbf{Role of Rhythm-Aware Reranking.}
The rhythm-aware reranking mechanism improves structural consistency by favoring 
candidates that better match target syllable counts and rhyme patterns. 
While constraints are not enforced during token-level decoding, reranking provides 
a flexible post-generation mechanism that enhances rhythmic quality without sacrificing 
fluency.

\textbf{Limitations of Emotion Mapping.}
The GoEmotions-to-FER mapping introduces information loss due to the aggregation 
of fine-grained emotional categories into a small set of basic emotions. As a result, 
semantically distinct emotions may be conflated during labeling. For example, 
expressions of affection or dominant love may be mapped to the angry class due to 
overlap with intensity-related categories, while narrative or descriptive lines with 
low emotional intensity may be incorrectly assigned to the sad or neutral classes. 
This many-to-one mapping can introduce label noise, particularly in cases where emotional 
nuance is important, such as distinguishing between reflective sadness and neutral narration.

\textbf{Ablation Insight: Emotion Mapping.}
Reassigning the disgust category to the angry group improves classification 
balance and reduces misalignment between textual and facial emotion representations, 
supporting the design choice of operating within a six-class emotion space.

\section{Conclusion}
% ============================================================

This work introduced \textbf{MERGE}, a multimodal framework for generating 
emotion-conditioned and rhythm-aware song lyrics by integrating facial 
emotion recognition with controlled language generation. The proposed 
approach combines a scalable GoEmotions-to-FER annotation pipeline, a 
high-performing FER ensemble model, GPT-2 fine-tuning with emotion control 
tokens, and a rhythm-aware reranking mechanism guided by syllable and rhyme 
constraints.

Experimental results demonstrate that emotion-conditioned generation, when 
paired with a large-scale, emotion-aligned dataset, significantly improves 
lyric quality, achieving substantial gains in PHREM scores over both pretrained 
and Spotify-based baselines. The reranking strategy further enhances structural 
consistency without compromising linguistic fluency.

Overall, MERGE provides an effective framework for combining multimodal 
emotional signals with structured lyric generation, while highlighting 
challenges related to coarse-grained emotion mapping and the absence of 
strict constraint enforcement during decoding.

\section{Future Work}

Several directions can further improve the MERGE framework. First, the current 
GoEmotions-to-FER mapping could be extended to preserve finer-grained emotional 
distinctions, reducing information loss and improving alignment between textual 
and visual emotion representations. Second, the rhythm-aware reranking mechanism 
can be strengthened by incorporating stricter constraint enforcement during generation, 
such as token-level control or beam-based structured decoding.

Additionally, the Rhyme Consistency Score (RCS) can be enhanced to model more 
complex rhyme structures. While the current formulation captures direct phonetic 
similarity between consecutive line endings, it does not account for global rhyme 
schemes (e.g., AABB, ABAB) or slant rhymes. Extending RCS to incorporate rhyme scheme 
modeling and approximate phonetic similarity would provide a more comprehensive evaluation 
of lyrical structure.

Finally, scaling the language model and incorporating richer multimodal inputs, such as audio 
or prosodic features, could further improve the quality and expressiveness of generated lyrics.
